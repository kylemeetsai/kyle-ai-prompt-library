AI OUTREACH AGENT
60 second setup
Scans your email and chat from the past 7 days, finds every thread where you sent the last message and got no reply, ranks them by what matters, and writes follow-up drafts you can send today. Can run automatically every Monday.
How to use
1. Tap Copy prompt below
2. Paste into a new Claude chat with your email and chat connectors set up (Gmail, Outlook, Slack, or Teams)
3. Answer a few quick questions about who and what matters most
4. Get ranked follow-ups and ready-to-send drafts
You are helping me set up a weekly follow up radar. The goal is simple. Find every email and chat thread from the past 7 days where I sent the last message and the other person has not replied, then rank them so I know which ones really matter and what to actually send today. I am new to this, so be patient. Walk me through it in four parts. Use plain language. Do not assume I know any technical terms. Do not use em dashes anywhere in your responses or in any drafts you write for me. Keep the tone professional but warm, like a thoughtful colleague. PART 1: CHECK MY CONNECTIONS Before scanning anything, look at the tools available to you in this chat. I am specifically looking for two things: 1. An email connector. Gmail or Outlook are the most common. 2. A chat connector. Slack or Microsoft Teams are the most common. Tell me clearly which of these you can see. If I have at least one email source and at least one chat source connected, say so and move to Part 2. If something is missing, stop. Do not try to scan yet. Instead: - Explain in one or two friendly sentences what a connector is and why we need at least one email source and one chat source. - Use any connector search or suggestion tools you have to recommend the specific ones I should add (Gmail, Outlook, Slack, Teams). - Give me simple instructions on how to add them, and tell me to come back when I have at least one email and one chat connected. - Wait for me to confirm I am set up before going further. If I only have one of the two (email or chat but not both), ask me whether I want to keep going with just what I have, or pause and add the other one. Respect my choice either way. PART 2: LEARN WHAT MATTERS TO ME Once I am connected, ask me these questions one at a time. Wait for my answer before moving to the next one. 1. Who are the people whose follow-ups should always be high priority for me? Examples: my boss, specific clients, agency partners, recruiters, board members, key teammates. List names if you can think of them. 2. What kinds of messages count as a real ask worth surfacing? Examples: a decision I am waiting on, an approval, a meeting that needs scheduling, an intro I requested, a question someone asked me. 3. Anything I want you to ignore on sight? Examples: newsletters, friends and family, casual office banter, recruiter spam, anything from specific senders. 4. How many follow-ups do I want to see at most? The default is five. I can say more or fewer. 5. Where do I want the drafts to land? Pick one: a. In a clean Word document I can read and copy from. b. As actual draft emails or unsent chat messages inside my connected tools, wherever your tools support that. c. Both. After I answer, repeat back what you heard in two or three sentences and ask me to confirm before you start scanning. If I want to change something, let me change it. PART 3: RUN THE SCAN AND WRITE THE DRAFTS Once I confirm, do the following: 1. Look at my sent email and chat messages from the last 7 days. 2. Find every thread where I sent the latest message and no one has replied yet. 3. Drop the noise. Skip newsletters, automated alerts, calendar notifications, bounce messages, casual chatter with no question, and anything where I was the one closing the loop. Also skip anything I told you to ignore in Part 2. 4. Score what is left on these signals: - How important the recipient is to me, using my list from Part 2. - Whether my message had a real ask, decision pending, or blocked next step. - Whether the topic is time sensitive (deadline, event, launch, contract window). - How long it has been silent. Longer silence on a real ask means higher priority. - Whether the relationship or project is actively moving versus a cold reach out. 5. Rank everything and split into HIGH PRIORITY (send today) and MEDIUM PRIORITY (send this week). 6. For the top items, up to the number I gave you in Part 2, write a follow-up draft I could actually send today. Each draft must: - Match the warmth and register of my original message. Read the original before drafting. - Skip every version of "just checking in," "circling back," "bumping this," and "wanted to follow up." These read as filler and lower the chance of a reply. - Give the other person a concrete reason to write back. Restate the specific decision they owe me, offer a new option, share a small piece of new information, or propose a time. - Stay short. Usually two to four sentences unless the original thread was longer or more formal. - Use the recipient's name the way I used it in the original thread. - Have no em dashes. Use commas, periods, or "and" instead. Keep the voice human. 7. Deliver the result in the format I picked in Part 2: - If I asked for a document, save a Word document in my workspace named "followup-radar-YYYY-MM-DD" with today's date, and share the link. - If I asked for in-platform drafts, create the draft emails and unsent chat messages directly using the connector tools that support it. Tell me clearly which platform each draft is sitting in so I know where to find them. - If I asked for both, do both. Open with a one sentence summary like "You have three high priority and two medium priority follow-ups waiting on you." If nothing is worth surfacing, say so plainly in one sentence. Do not invent threads or fabricate context. If a thread is ambiguous, leave it out instead of guessing. PART 4: MAKE IT A WEEKLY HABIT After the first run is done, ask me if I want this to run automatically every Monday at 8am in my local timezone. If I say yes, create a scheduled task that runs the exact same process every Monday morning using the same criteria I gave you in Part 2. Tell me where to find the scheduled task and how to edit or pause it later if my priorities change. If I say no, leave it as a one time thing. I can always ask for it again. NOW BEGIN WITH PART 1. Check what is connected and tell me where I stand.
